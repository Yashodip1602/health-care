Diabetes

What is diabetes?

Diabetes mellitus is a chronic condition in which blood glucose (sugar) levels are too high. Glucose is a primary source of energy, but it needs the hormone insulin to enter the body's cells. Diabetes occurs when there is a problem with insulin production or how the body uses it.

There are several types of diabetes:

  • Type 1 diabetes:
    An autoimmune disease where the body mistakenly attacks and destroys the insulin-producing cells in the pancreas. This means little to no insulin is produced, and it often appears suddenly.
  • Type 2 diabetes:
    A metabolic disorder where the body either doesn't produce enough insulin or develops "insulin resistance," meaning the body's cells don't respond to insulin effectively. It is the most common type and often develops gradually.
  • Prediabetes:
    A condition where blood sugar levels are higher than normal but not high enough for a diabetes diagnosis. Early intervention can help reverse this condition.
  • Gestational diabetes:
    Occurs during pregnancy due to hormonal changes that cause insulin resistance. It usually resolves after childbirth but increases the risk of developing type 2 diabetes later.

How to check for diabetes

Diabetes is diagnosed with blood tests performed by a doctor, not with at-home glucometers. In India, labs like Dr Lal PathLabs and Tata 1mg Labs offer testing services, sometimes with at-home sample collection.

The main diagnostic blood tests include:

  • A1C test (or HbA1C):
    A1C test (or HbA1C):
    • Normal:
      Below 5.7%
    • Prediabetes:
      5.7% to 6.4%
    • Diabetes:
      6.5% or higher on two separate tests
  • Fasting Plasma Glucose (FPG) test:
    Measures blood sugar after an overnight fast (at least 8 hours).
    • Normal:
      99 mg/dL or below
    • Prediabetes:
      100 to 125 mg/dL
    • Diabetes:
      126 mg/dL or higher on two separate tests
  • Random Plasma Glucose (RPG) test:
    Measures blood sugar at any time. A reading of 200 mg/dL or higher in a person with symptoms suggests diabetes, but a confirmatory test is usually needed.
  • Oral Glucose Tolerance Test (OGTT):
    Measures blood sugar before and two hours after consuming a sugary drink. A reading of 200 mg/dL or higher after two hours suggests diabetes.

Physical work and diabetes

Physical activity is extremely important for managing diabetes. It helps control blood sugar levels, improves insulin sensitivity, and lowers the risk of complications.

  • Recommended activity:
    Aim for at least 150 minutes of moderate aerobic activity (brisk walking, swimming, cycling) each week, spread over several days. Also, include muscle-strengthening activities 2–3 times a week.
  • Check with your doctor
    Consult your doctor before starting any new exercise program, especially if you have pre-existing health conditions like diabetic retinopathy.
  • Check blood sugar:
    Monitor your blood sugar levels before and after exercise, especially if you take insulin. Carry a fast-acting carbohydrate source like glucose tablets or juice to treat low blood sugar if needed.

Cardiovascular Diseases

What are cardiovascular diseases?

Cardiovascular diseases (CVD) are a group of disorders involving the heart and blood vessels. They include conditions such as coronary artery disease, stroke, heart failure, and hypertension. Risk factors include high blood pressure, high cholesterol, smoking, diabetes, obesity, and a sedentary lifestyle.

There are several major types of CVD:

  • Coronary artery disease (CAD):
    Narrowing or blockage of the arteries supplying the heart, which can cause chest pain (angina) or heart attack.
  • Stroke:
    Disruption of blood flow to the brain, causing brain injury. Strokes can be ischemic (blocked artery) or hemorrhagic (bleeding).
  • Heart failure:
    A condition where the heart cannot pump blood effectively to meet the body's needs.
  • Hypertension (high blood pressure):
    Persistent elevation of blood pressure that increases the risk of heart disease and stroke.
  • Arrhythmias:
    Abnormal heart rhythms that can affect how well the heart pumps blood.
  • Peripheral artery disease (PAD):
    Narrowing of arteries outside the heart, often in the legs, causing leg pain and ulcers.

How to assess cardiovascular risk

Regular evaluation by a healthcare provider helps identify risk factors and guide prevention. Key measures include blood pressure, cholesterol levels, blood glucose, body weight, and lifestyle assessment.

Common diagnostic tests for CVD:

  • Blood pressure measurement:
    Determines if blood pressure is within normal range.
  • Lipid profile:
    Measures cholesterol and triglyceride levels to assess heart disease risk.
  • Electrocardiogram (ECG):
    Records the electrical activity of the heart to detect arrhythmias and heart muscle problems.
  • Echocardiography:
    Uses ultrasound to visualize heart structure and function.
  • Stress testing:
    Assesses how the heart performs under physical exertion.
  • Imaging (CT/MRI):
    Evaluates blood vessels and heart anatomy when indicated.

Lifestyle and cardiovascular health

Adopting healthy habits helps prevent and manage CVD. This includes regular physical activity, a heart-healthy diet, maintaining a healthy weight, avoiding tobacco, and limiting alcohol.

  • Recommended activity:
    Aim for at least 150 minutes of moderate aerobic activity (brisk walking, cycling) each week, plus 2–3 sessions of strength training.
  • Dietary choices:
    Emphasize vegetables, fruits, whole grains, lean proteins, and healthy fats; limit saturated fats, salt, and added sugars.
  • Medical management:
    Follow prescribed treatments for high blood pressure, high cholesterol, diabetes, or other conditions that raise CVD risk.

Chronic Respiratory Diseases

What are chronic respiratory diseases?

Chronic respiratory diseases (CRDs) are long-lasting conditions that affect the airways and lungs. Common CRDs include chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD), asthma, bronchiectasis, and interstitial lung diseases. These conditions can cause breathing difficulties, coughing, and reduced exercise tolerance.

There are several major types of CRDs:

  • Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD):
    Progressive airflow limitation usually caused by smoking or exposure to irritants.
  • Asthma:
    Reversible airway inflammation leading to wheezing, shortness of breath, and coughing.
  • Bronchiectasis:
    Irreversible widening of airways with mucus buildup and frequent infections.
  • Interstitial lung diseases (ILDs):
    A group of disorders causing scarring (fibrosis) of lung tissue.
  • Pulmonary hypertension:
    Elevated blood pressure in the lungs' arteries, affecting heart and lung function.

How to assess respiratory health

Regular evaluation by a healthcare provider helps identify symptoms, triggers, and disease control. Key measures include lung function tests, symptom scoring, imaging, and exercise tolerance assessment.

Common diagnostic tests for CRDs:

  • Spirometry:
    Measures how much and how quickly you can move air in and out of the lungs.
  • Chest X-ray or CT scan:
    Visualizes lung structure and detects anomalies or scarring.
  • Lung diffusion capacity (DLCO):
    Assesses how well gases exchange in the lungs.
  • Arterial blood gas (ABG):
    Evaluates oxygen and carbon dioxide levels in the blood.
  • Exercise testing:
    Assesses oxygen needs and capacity during physical activity.
  • Allergy testing or imaging for ILDs:
    Helps identify triggers or causes of specific CRDs.

Lifestyle and respiratory health

Managing CRDs involves avoiding triggers, adhering to medications, vaccination, and maintaining overall health. Avoid tobacco, minimize air irritants, and use inhalers or other prescribed therapies as directed.

  • Medication adherence:
    Use inhalers, tablets, or nebulizers as prescribed to control symptoms and flare-ups.
  • Vaccinations:
    Stay up to date with influenza and pneumococcal vaccines to reduce respiratory infections.
  • Pulmonary rehabilitation:
    A structured program combining exercise and education to improve breathing.

Alzheimer's disease and other dementias

What are Alzheimer's disease and other dementias?

Alzheimer's disease is a progressive neurological disorder that causes memory loss and cognitive decline. Other dementias include vascular dementia, Lewy body dementia, frontotemporal dementia, and mixed dementia. These conditions impair thinking, behavior, and daily functioning over time.

There are several major types of dementia:

  • Alzheimer's disease:
    The most common form of dementia, characterized by progressive memory loss and cognitive impairment.
  • Vascular dementia:
    Dementia caused by reduced blood flow to the brain, often after a stroke or series of mini-strokes.
  • Lewy body dementia:
    Involves abnormal protein deposits in the brain leading to visual hallucinations, movement problems, and fluctuations in alertness.
  • Frontotemporal dementia:
    Affects the frontal and temporal lobes, leading to changes in personality, behavior, and language.
  • Mixed dementia:
    A combination of dementia types, most commonly Alzheimer’s disease with vascular dementia.

How to assess cognitive health

Evaluation by a healthcare professional includes medical history, cognitive and functional assessments, physical examination, and often imaging or laboratory tests to rule out other causes of cognitive symptoms.

Common diagnostic tools for dementia:

  • Cognitive screening tests:
    Tools like the Mini-Mental State Examination (MMSE) or Montreal Cognitive Assessment (MoCA) assess memory and thinking skills.
  • Comprehensive neuropsychological testing:
    In-depth assessment of memory, language, attention, and problem-solving abilities.
  • Brain imaging:
    MRI or CT scans detect structural changes, strokes, tumors, or other causes.
  • Laboratory tests:
    Blood tests to identify reversible causes of cognitive decline (e.g., thyroid, vitamin deficiencies, infections).
  • Neuropathological assessment (post-mortem):
    Used in research to confirm dementia type by examining brain tissue.

Lifestyle and brain health

While some dementias have a genetic component, many risk factors can be addressed through lifestyle and medical care. Engage in mentally stimulating activities, physical activity, social engagement, and cardiovascular risk management.

  • Physical activity:
    Aim for regular aerobic and strength-training activities as advised by a clinician.
  • Dietary choices:
    Emphasize Mediterranean-style diets rich in vegetables, fruits, whole grains, fish, and healthy fats.
  • Cardiovascular risk management:
    Control blood pressure, cholesterol, diabetes, and maintain a healthy weight.

Kidney Diseases

What are kidney diseases?

Kidney diseases refer to conditions that affect kidney structure or function. They can range from acute kidney injury to chronic kidney disease (CKD) and end-stage renal disease (ESRD). Kidneys help filter waste, balance fluids and electrolytes, and regulate blood pressure.

There are several major types of kidney diseases:

  • Chronic kidney disease (CKD):
    Gradual loss of kidney function over months to years.
  • Acute kidney injury (AKI):
    Sudden decrease in kidney function, often reversible with prompt treatment.
  • Glomerulonephritis:
    Inflammation of the kidney filtering units (glomeruli) that can affect filtration.
  • Polycystic kidney disease (PKD):
    A genetic disorder characterized by growth of numerous cysts in the kidneys.
  • Kidney stones:
    Hard mineral deposits that form in the kidneys and can cause pain and obstruction.

How to assess kidney health

Evaluation by a healthcare provider includes assessing symptoms, urine tests, blood tests, and imaging to determine kidney function and structure. Early detection improves outcomes.

Common diagnostic tests for kidney disease:

  • Estimated Glomerular Filtration Rate (eGFR):
    Measures kidney filtration function using blood creatinine levels.
  • Urinalysis:
    Detects protein, blood, sugar, and other substances in urine.
  • Albumin-to-creatinine ratio (ACR):
    Assesses urine protein loss, an early sign of kidney disease.
  • Blood tests:
    Check electrolytes, kidney waste products (creatinine, urea), and minerals.
  • Renal imaging:
    Ultrasound, CT, or MRI to visualize kidney size, structure, and stones.
  • Kidney biopsy:
    May be indicated to determine specific causes of kidney disease.

Lifestyle and kidney health

Protecting kidney health involves controlling blood pressure and blood sugar, staying hydrated, limiting nephrotoxic medications, and adopting a kidney-friendly diet.

  • Dietary choices:
    Limit salt, processed foods, and high-phosphorus items; choose fruits, vegetables, and lean proteins as advised.
  • Blood pressure management:
    Maintain blood pressure within target ranges to reduce kidney damage risk.
  • Glycemic control:
    If diabetic, maintain good blood sugar control to slow CKD progression.
  • Hydration and medications:
    Use medications as prescribed and avoid overuse of NSAIDs and other nephrotoxic substances.
